Industry 4.0

The fourth industrial revolution, commonly known as Industry 4.0, arose from new and revolutionary intelligence and information technologies. These innovative technologies enable ever-increasing levels of industrial efficiency (Bai et al., 2020). In this global and interconnected Industry 4.0 scenario, 100 percent company security is impossible. Cybersecurity technology, such as encryption and machine-learning approaches, are developing rapidly, however the problem is far broader. Many standards have been issued throughout the years, each recommending a separate set of cybersecurity procedures, strategies, protocols, and evaluation tools. The ISO/IEC 27001 on information security management systems released by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the NIST cybersecurity framework supported by the US National Institute of Standards and Technology are the most prominent of these IT standards (Culot et al., 2019). in my initial post, mostly targeted companies in Ukraine, Russia, and Europe. NotPetya, like WannaCry, used a modified version of the EternalBlue vulnerability, as well as an Eternal Romance exploit publicly disclosed by the Shadow Brokers. The malware spread through an upgrade to MeDoc (a Ukrainian accounting application) (Mansfield-Devine, 2017). NotPetya severely harmed Maersk's dynamic host configuration protocol (DHCP) and Active Directory services, wrecked the enterprise service bus, and harmed the vCenter, which manages the cloud. In terms of end-user electronics, 49,000 laptops were destroyed, along with all print capability and file shares. All of Maersk's 1,200 apps were destroyed completely, and about 1,000 were deleted, while around 3,500 of 6,200 servers were damaged (Ashford, 2019). Several conclusions can be taken from the instances described in the previous article and peer posts. Despite the efforts of governments, organisations, and businesses to raise industry awareness of cyber-threats and their implications, many of today's industrial networks are still vulnerable to cyber-attack.

GDPR

The Data Protection Act 2018 is the UK's implementation of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Everyone responsible for using personal data must follow strict rules called 'data protection principles'. They must make sure the information is: used fairly, lawfully, and transparently.

While studying this Act within the Cyber security scop, I read several case studies on "https://dataprotection.ie/en/pre-gdpr/case-studies". Below I have discussed one of the incidents happened in 2017, that demonstrate how and why this GDPR act is important in Cyber security.

Case Study 2017 (5) - Disclosure of sensitive personal data by a hospital to a third party

1. What is the specific aspect of GDPR that your case study addresses?

Data Protection Acts 1988 and 2003:

  • Section 2(1)(b) (requirement to keep personal data accurate, complete, and up to date),
  • Section 2(1)(d) (requirement to take appropriate security measures)
  • Section 2B (1) (requirement for a legal basis for processing sensitive personal data)

    • 2. How was it resolved? 

    The hospital's apologies and offer of a peaceful settlement were refused by the complaining party, who instead requested the Commissioner for a formal judgement. The Commissioner stated that the hospital had poorly handled the patient's sensitive personal data by accidentally revealing them to a third party, in violation of Sections 2(1)(b), 2(1)(d), and Section 2B (1) of the Data Protection Acts 1988 and 2003.

    3. If this was your organisation, what steps would you take as an Information Security Manager to mitigate the issue?

    The foundation for responsible medical practise and a key component of doctor-patient trust is confidentiality. However, it is also the most common risk for patients. As the hospital's information security manager, my initial step to mitigate this risk is to organise regular staff training sessions where I explain the value of data confidentiality and the staff's responsibilities, raise their awareness of current government regulations related to patient data confidentiality, instruct them to spot possible confidentiality breaches at early stages, and investigate ways to lower risks. In addition, 

  • Using a secure platform combined with security protocols like encryption, two-factor authentication, and storage on secured data centres with a limited access to the general staff.
  • Keeping physical patient’s files, medical records, treatment reports in a secure room with controlled access. Make sure that the authorized team members never leave documents with unauthorized persons. Keep documents correctly labelled and stored in correct shelves.
  • Conduct routine security checks of the procedures, using up-to-Date security technology to share documents with the patients. 
  • When sharing with third parties, always ask for patient’s consent (using consent forms).
  • After a certain period/ when the treatments are finished/ if the patient no longer taking treatments, return all confidential information to the patient, or destroy them in a secured manner. 
  • When sending through post/ physical documents – Double check the patient’s address, use reliable methods like signed and registered post or courier. 
    • When sending soft copies online - using password protected files, Encryptions.

    CVSS 

    The Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) is a method used to supply a qualitative measure of severity.

    The most modern method for evaluating software vulnerabilities is the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS). Base metrics, Temporal metrics, and Environmental metrics are the three categories of metrics used to generate vulnerability ratings (Mell and Scarfone, 2007). However, as we have previously discussed, it has come under fire for lacking professional relevance and reliability. Spring et al. (2021) argues that neither formally nor practically, the CVSS scoring method cannot be justified. The CVSS v3.0 documentation makes no claims about how reliable the construction process or formula is. The calculation process is straightforward but unjustified. CVSS, on the other hand, was created to identify the technical significance of a vulnerability. The risk that a vulnerability or flaw brings to them or how promptly they ought to react to a vulnerability are what people seem to be interested in instead. If so, CVSS must be improved, or a new system must be implemented by the community. Relying on CVSS for vulnerability management is going to result in security teams wasting time and money on patch cycles that prioritise technically challenging vulnerabilities that don't offer the greatest threat to the environment and focus on low-impact, low-probability problems. rather than relying simply on CVSS scores, security professionals need to identify reliable solutions that consider their unique environment and the complex nature of the real world (Spring et al., 2021). Along with CVSS criticisms, vulnerability management frameworks have been developing in recent years. Several initiatives have been made to give risk-informed prioritisation by considering exploitation probability context. These are the Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (SSVC) and the Exploit Prediction Scoring System (EPSS) (Tamagna-Darr, 2023). 

    The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) and Carnegie Mellon University's Software Engineering Institute jointly created the vulnerability analysis methodology known as Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (SSVC). The fundamental SSVC technique considers a vulnerability's exploitation state, effects on security, and frequency of the affected device (Tamagna-Darr, 2023). The Exploit Prediction Scoring System (EPSS) is an open, statistical approach of evaluating the probability that a software vulnerability will be exploited in the environment. The purpose of EPSS is to give ability to prioritise patching vulnerabilities more effectively by utilising up-to-date threat information from actual attack data. The risk that a vulnerability will be exploited is expressed by a probability score that ranges from 0 to 1 (Tamagna-Darr, 2023).
